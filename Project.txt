MALARIA AND TYPHOID HEALTH ASSISTANT MOBILE APPLICATION

CHAPTER ONE
INTRODUCTION

1.0 Background of the Study
Malaria and typhoid fever remain major public health concerns in many developing countries, especially in communities with limited access to timely health information, early screening support, and nearby medical facilities. Delays in identifying symptoms, poor adherence to medication schedules, and low awareness of preventive practices contribute to avoidable complications and mortality.

At the same time, smartphone usage has increased significantly, creating an opportunity to deliver health education and decision-support tools directly to users. Mobile health (mHealth) applications can bridge information gaps by providing symptom guidance, health tips, medicine reminders, emergency contacts, and location-based access to nearby hospitals.

This study presents the design and implementation of a mobile Malaria and Typhoid Health Assistant built with React Native and Expo. The application is designed as an offline-first system with practical features such as a rule-based symptom checker, educational content, reminders, and hospital navigation support. The system is not intended to replace professional diagnosis but to improve awareness, promote early action, and support healthier behavior.

1.1 Statement of the Problem
Many people in malaria- and typhoid-prone environments face the following problems:
1. Poor access to accurate and timely disease education.
2. Late response to early warning symptoms.
3. Non-adherence to medication schedules due to forgetfulness.
4. Difficulty locating nearby healthcare facilities quickly.
5. Dependence on internet connectivity for health resources in low-connectivity areas.

These issues increase the risk of disease progression, improper self-medication, and delayed treatment.

1.2 Aim of the Study
The aim of this study is to develop a user-friendly mobile health assistant that supports malaria and typhoid awareness, symptom self-screening, medication reminder management, and healthcare access guidance.

1.3 Objectives of the Study
The objectives are to:
1. Design and implement a rule-based symptom checker for malaria and typhoid risk indication.
2. Provide accessible educational content and preventive health tips.
3. Develop a reminder module for medication and wellness tracking.
4. Implement emergency contact and nearby hospital support.
5. Build an offline-first mobile system suitable for low-resource settings.
6. Evaluate the functionality and usability of the proposed application.

1.4 Research Questions
1. How can a mobile application improve awareness of malaria and typhoid symptoms and prevention?
2. To what extent can a rule-based symptom checker support early decision-making before hospital visitation?
3. Can medication reminders within a mobile app improve adherence behavior?
4. How effective is an offline-first design for users with unstable internet connectivity?

1.5 Hypothesis
H0 (Null Hypothesis): The developed mobile application has no significant effect on users' awareness and self-management behavior regarding malaria and typhoid.

H1 (Alternative Hypothesis): The developed mobile application significantly improves users' awareness and self-management behavior regarding malaria and typhoid.

1.6 Significance/Justification of the Study
This study is significant because it:
1. Contributes to digital health solutions for communicable disease awareness.
2. Supports individuals with practical tools for early symptom response.
3. Assists patients in remembering medications through reminders.
4. Improves access to healthcare navigation by showing hospitals and directions.
5. Demonstrates the feasibility of a low-cost, offline-first mHealth intervention for local communities.
6. Provides a useful reference for future research in health informatics and mobile computing.

1.7 Scope of the Study
The study covers the design, development, and testing of a mobile app with the following modules:
1. Onboarding and disclaimer interface.
2. Rule-based malaria/typhoid symptom checker.
3. Health education and prevention tips.
4. Daily reminder setup and management.
5. Nearby hospitals (location-aware with offline fallback list).
6. Contacts and profile/settings interfaces.

The system is targeted at smartphone users and is implemented with React Native (Expo). It does not include laboratory diagnosis, doctor-patient teleconsultation, or a full cloud-based medical record system.

1.8 Limitation of the Study
1. The symptom checker is rule-based and cannot replace clinical diagnosis.
2. Notification behavior depends on device permissions and platform constraints.
3. Live hospital search requires location permission and external map services.
4. The app currently uses local/offline data; real-time epidemiological feeds are not integrated.
5. The study does not include large-scale clinical validation.

1.10 Definition of Terms
1. Malaria: A mosquito-borne infectious disease caused by Plasmodium parasites.
2. Typhoid Fever: A bacterial infection caused by Salmonella Typhi, often spread through contaminated food or water.
3. mHealth: Use of mobile devices and applications for health services and information.
4. Symptom Checker: A digital tool that estimates risk level based on user-reported symptoms.
5. Offline-First: A design approach where key app features work without continuous internet access.
6. Reminder Notification: Automated alert generated by the app at predefined times.


CHAPTER TWO
LITERATURE REVIEW

2.0 Introduction
This chapter reviews existing literature related to mobile health interventions, symptom assessment systems, medication adherence tools, and digital support for malaria control. The review establishes the conceptual and theoretical foundations for the proposed system and identifies research gaps addressed by this study.

2.1 Conceptual Framework
The conceptual model of this study links mobile health intervention components to expected health-support outcomes.

Input Components:
1. User symptom responses.
2. Local educational and prevention content.
3. Reminder time entries.
4. User location data (when granted).

System Processes:
1. Rule-based symptom scoring and risk categorization.
2. Storage and retrieval of reminders/contacts.
3. Notification scheduling.
4. Hospital listing and map-based navigation.

Output/Outcome Components:
1. Increased awareness of malaria and typhoid signs.
2. Improved medication adherence behavior.
3. Faster decision to seek professional care.
4. Better access to nearby healthcare facilities.

2.2 Theoretical Framework
Two theories guide this work:

1. Health Belief Model (HBM)
HBM explains that individuals are more likely to engage in health actions when they perceive disease susceptibility, severity, benefits of action, and minimal barriers. The app supports these constructs through symptom awareness prompts, prevention education, and actionable reminders.

2. Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
TAM states that perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use determine technology adoption. The app emphasizes simple navigation, clear interfaces, and practical functions (symptom checker, reminders, hospital access) to encourage sustained use.

2.3 Empirical Framework
Empirical studies on mHealth in low- and middle-income settings indicate that mobile interventions can improve health-seeking behavior, treatment adherence, and communication efficiency. SMS/app-based reminders have shown positive influence on adherence outcomes, while mobile surveillance and decision-support tools improve timeliness of health responses. However, many existing solutions are either disease-specific with limited usability scope or heavily dependent on connectivity. This study addresses these gaps by combining multiple practical health-support features in an offline-first mobile architecture.

2.4 Review of Related Work
Prior studies reveal strong potential for mobile technologies in disease control and patient support. Some systems focus on provider-side surveillance, others on patient education and reminders. A recurring limitation is fragmentation: users often need separate tools for symptom checks, education, reminders, and facility access. The present study integrates these into a single application targeted at malaria and typhoid awareness and self-management.

CHAPTER THREE
METHODOLOGY

3.0 Introduction
This chapter presents the procedures used to analyze the existing problem, design the proposed system, implement the mobile application, and test its functionality.

3.1 Research Design
The study adopted a Design and Development Research approach, suitable for building and evaluating a software artifact. A practical iterative model was used:
1. Requirement identification.
2. Interface and logic design.
3. Implementation.
4. Testing and refinement.

3.2 Population of the Study
The target population includes smartphone users in malaria- and typhoid-prone environments, especially students, workers, and caregivers who require quick health guidance, reminders, and nearby care support.

3.4 Sample and Sampling Procedure
For usability-oriented evaluation, a purposive sample can be drawn from intended end users familiar with Android/iOS devices. Participants should represent varied age groups and basic digital literacy levels to assess accessibility and practicality of the interface.

3.5 Instrument
The instruments used include:
1. Functional requirement checklist.
2. Test cases for each app module.
3. Observation-based usability checklist.
4. Device-level runtime testing on mobile emulator/physical device.

3.6 Methods

Analysis of the Existing System Data
The existing condition was analyzed through observation of common user pain points:
1. Fragmented access to disease information.
2. Lack of quick self-screening guidance.
3. Inconsistent medication reminders.
4. Delayed discovery of nearby health facilities.

System Design
The proposed system was designed using a modular architecture:
1. Presentation Layer: React Native screens and reusable UI components.
2. Logic Layer: Rule-based symptom scoring, reminder scheduling logic, and location handling.
3. Data Layer: Local JSON datasets and local storage for user reminders/contacts.

Major modules:
1. Home dashboard and quick actions.
2. Symptom checker (malaria/typhoid risk scoring).
3. Education and health tips.
4. Reminder manager with notification support.
5. Hospital map/list with offline fallback.

System Programming/Construction
The application was implemented with:
1. React Native + Expo Router for cross-platform navigation.
2. TypeScript for typed and maintainable code.
3. Expo Notifications for reminder alarms.
4. AsyncStorage and local state patterns for persistence.
5. React Native Maps and Expo Location for nearby hospital mapping.

System Testing
Testing was conducted at module and integration levels:
1. Symptom checker test: Verified score computation and risk output for various answer combinations.
2. Reminder test: Verified create/delete flow, time validation, and alarm scheduling where supported.
3. Education test: Verified content rendering from local data.
4. Hospital test: Verified permission handling, map marker display, fallback hospital list, and direction links.
5. Usability test: Verified navigation clarity, responsiveness, and consistency across screens.


CHAPTER FOUR
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

4.0 Introduction
This chapter presents the implementation outcomes and discusses how the developed system addresses the identified problems.

4.1 Results
The developed mobile application successfully delivered the planned features:
1. Symptom Checker:
	- Multi-step question flow.
	- Rule-based scoring for malaria and typhoid indicators.
	- Risk-level output with advice (low, moderate, high).

2. Education Module:
	- Structured disease education and prevention guidance.
	- Readable content cards suitable for everyday users.

3. Reminder Module:
	- User-defined reminder text and time.
	- Daily notification scheduling on supported devices.
	- Clear handling for unsupported environments and permission limits.

4. Hospital Module:
	- Geolocation-based nearby facility retrieval where available.
	- Offline fallback list of hospitals when network/API access fails.
	- Direction launch via map links.

5. General Interface Outcome:
	- Modern and consistent UI.
	- Quick-access dashboard for major tasks.
	- Functional cross-screen navigation.

4.2 Discussion of Results
The results indicate that a lightweight offline-first mHealth app can provide meaningful support for malaria and typhoid awareness and self-management. The symptom checker encourages early health-seeking decisions by transforming symptom responses into understandable risk feedback. Educational content reinforces preventive behavior, while reminders address one of the major behavioral barriers: forgetfulness.

The hospital module strengthens practical utility by connecting users to nearby care options and retaining fallback functionality when connectivity is poor. These outcomes align with prior mHealth findings that emphasize usability, accessibility, and actionable prompts as drivers of impact.

However, because risk scoring is rule-based, the system should be treated as an awareness and guidance tool, not a diagnostic authority. Future versions can include stronger personalization, multilingual content, clinician collaboration workflows, and broader validation studies.


CHAPTER FIVE
SUMMARY OF FINDINGS, CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION

5.0 Introduction
This chapter summarizes key findings from the study, presents the conclusion, and offers recommendations for improvement and future work.

5.1 Summary of Findings
The study found that:
1. A mobile-first health assistant can consolidate education, symptom guidance, reminders, and hospital access into a single platform.
2. Rule-based symptom assessment provides useful early-risk orientation for users.
3. Reminder functionality enhances medication and routine adherence support.
4. Offline-first design improves usability in low-connectivity environments.
5. Integrated health utility features improve user convenience compared to fragmented tools.

5.2 Conclusion
This study achieved its aim by designing and implementing a functional Malaria and Typhoid Health Assistant mobile application. The system addresses key public health support needs at the user level through awareness content, symptom risk indication, reminder scheduling, and healthcare location assistance. The application demonstrates that practical, low-cost mHealth systems can contribute to improved health behavior and earlier care-seeking decisions, especially in settings with limited access to continuous internet services.

5.3 Recommendations
1. Integrate optional backend synchronization for secure user data backup.
2. Extend language support to local languages for wider accessibility.
3. Add clinician-reviewed content updates and periodic guideline refresh.
4. Incorporate analytics for anonymized usage insights and impact tracking.
5. Conduct broader user studies and, where possible, clinical validation partnerships.
6. Add AI-assisted triage only under strict medical governance and safety controls.
